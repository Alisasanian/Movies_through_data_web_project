Exploring movies through data

Film advice was started as an attempt to bring better movie recommendations to people that are looking for them. The idea of recommending someone with a piece of entertainment they would like to watch, has long been an attractive topic of exploration due to its capital and social importance in this era. But has often been pursuid using the data collected from viewers, rather than providing them with data to facilitate the decision making process.

 

According to The Wrap, a new study by Reelgood and Learndipity Data Insights found Netflix users spend on average 18 minutes on a given day deciding what to watch, or twice as much as cable television viewers. Netflix patrons also spend more days watching entertainment per week, but watch fewer programs per day as cable.

 

I believe the idea that we have genres to pick from and categories to choose from have become redundant to us and we do not relate to them anymore. The line between classical categorization of media such as movies vs tv shows, recently released vs outdated, horror vs drama, has long faded and we are left wondering why what we are looking for cannot be found.

 

Film advice uses data visualization to offer the viewer a new set of categories they could explore on their path to find their perfect piece of media they would like to interact with. When faced with an interactive data visualization that highlights factors that were not previously accessible on other streaming services, the user might find themselves interested in a specific actor/actress, or movies with a specific budget or director, which would then lead them to lookup movies from those directors or actors and find something they would like.

 

Well this might not have recommended them a movie, but has surely helped narrow down the field, which is often what we all really need. Somewhere to start looking from.

Some more serious remarks

 

Consumer spending on digital entertainment in the United States on digital distribution reached a record 30 billion U.S. dollars in 2020, up by over 21 percent from the previous year.

 

Similar to that, the largest media companies in the U.S. spent over 80 Billion dollars on content in 2019 and Netflix which was ranked 4th in 2019 is estimated to spend a massive 17 billion dollars on content in 2021. The 8 billion dollar increase in content spending of Netflix signifies a remarkable attempt at trying to capture the most number of viewers by one of the largest media companies in the world and as such, an attempt by all media companies of the same scale to compete for capturing the same viewers.

 

Film advice is an exploration into how we feel about the movies we like. Most movie recommendation algorithms rely on a large set of auctioned or collected data that was stored from millions of viewers to streamline a better recommendation set and at first, was indeed the building block of this project as well. But after looking into ways of developing this platform, I quickly came to the realization that this field could use a rather unconventional approach that would allow the viewers themselves to make an even more conscious decision about what they are going to watch.

 

Film advice is a platform that allows the viewer to scroll and interact with a set of data visualizations that could potentially inspire them to watch a specific movie or a movie that has a certain actor or director. The idea that “a fun interaction can inspire people” is what film advice relies on to potentially introduce the audience to a movie or tv show they would find entertaining.

 

At first glance, this might seem like a weak argument, but I hope that interacting with the visualizations could drastically change that opinion and allow you to get excited and look forward to finding something you would like to watch.
